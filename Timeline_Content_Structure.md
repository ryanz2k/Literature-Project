## PERIOD 1: ANCIENT LITERATURE (3000 BCE - 500 CE)

### Entry 1: The Epic of Gilgamesh
- **Time Period/Date:** c. 2100 BCE
- **Title:** The Epic of Gilgamesh
- **Author:** Anonymous (Sumerian/Babylonian)
- **Country/Region:** Mesopotamia (Modern-day Iraq)
- **Description:** The Epic of Gilgamesh is the oldest known work of literature in human history, written on clay tablets in cuneiform script. This epic poem tells the story of Gilgamesh, a legendary king of Uruk, and his quest for immortality after the death of his friend Enkidu. The work explores universal themes of friendship, mortality, and the human condition, making it remarkably relevant even today. It represents one of humanity's earliest attempts to understand life, death, and the meaning of existence through storytelling.
- **Image Suggestions:** Ancient cuneiform tablets, Mesopotamian art, Gilgamesh reliefs, ancient Babylonian artifacts

---

### Entry 2: Homer's The Iliad and The Odyssey
- **Time Period/Date:** c. 8th century BCE
- **Title:** The Iliad and The Odyssey
- **Author:** Homer (traditionally attributed)
- **Country/Region:** Ancient Greece
- **Description:** Homer's epic poems are foundational works of Western literature, originally composed orally and later written down. The Iliad recounts the Trojan War, focusing on the hero Achilles and themes of honor, glory, and wrath. The Odyssey follows Odysseus's ten-year journey home after the war, exploring themes of cunning, perseverance, and the longing for home. These works established many conventions of epic poetry and continue to influence literature, art, and culture worldwide.
- **Image Suggestions:** Ancient Greek pottery with Homeric scenes, Greek sculptures, classical art depicting Achilles or Odysseus, ancient Greek manuscripts

---

### Entry 3: The Mahabharata
- **Time Period/Date:** c. 400 BCE - 400 CE
- **Title:** The Mahabharata
- **Author:** Traditionally attributed to Vyasa
- **Country/Region:** Ancient India
- **Description:** The Mahabharata is one of the longest epic poems in world literature, containing over 200,000 verses. It tells the story of a great war between two branches of a royal family and includes the Bhagavad Gita, a sacred Hindu text. The epic explores complex themes of duty (dharma), righteousness, and the moral dilemmas faced in life. It has profoundly influenced Indian culture, philosophy, and literature for over two millennia and remains a living tradition in South Asia.
- **Image Suggestions:** Ancient Indian manuscripts, temple carvings depicting Mahabharata scenes, classical Indian art, traditional Indian illustrations

---

### Entry 4: Virgil's The Aeneid
- **Time Period/Date:** 29-19 BCE
- **Title:** The Aeneid
- **Author:** Virgil (Publius Vergilius Maro)
- **Country/Region:** Ancient Rome
- **Description:** The Aeneid is a Roman epic poem that tells the legendary story of Aeneas, a Trojan hero who travels to Italy and becomes the ancestor of the Romans. Written during the reign of Augustus, it was intended to provide Rome with a national epic comparable to Homer's works. The poem explores themes of duty, fate, and the founding of civilizations. It became a cornerstone of Roman literature and education, influencing countless later works including Dante's Divine Comedy.
- **Image Suggestions:** Roman sculptures, ancient Roman art, classical architecture, Roman manuscripts, depictions of Aeneas

---

## PERIOD 2: MEDIEVAL & RENAISSANCE LITERATURE (500 - 1600 CE)

### Entry 5: Beowulf
- **Time Period/Date:** c. 700-1000 CE
- **Title:** Beowulf
- **Author:** Anonymous (Anglo-Saxon poet)
- **Country/Region:** England/Anglo-Saxon
- **Description:** Beowulf is the oldest surviving epic poem in Old English and one of the most important works of Anglo-Saxon literature. The poem tells the story of Beowulf, a Geatish hero who battles three monsters: Grendel, Grendel's mother, and a dragon. It explores themes of heroism, loyalty, mortality, and the struggle between good and evil. Written in alliterative verse, Beowulf provides valuable insight into early English culture and has influenced modern fantasy literature, including J.R.R. Tolkien's works.
- **Image Suggestions:** Medieval manuscripts, Anglo-Saxon art, Viking/Norse imagery, illuminated manuscripts, medieval illustrations

---

### Entry 6: Dante Alighieri's The Divine Comedy
- **Time Period/Date:** 1308-1321 CE
- **Title:** The Divine Comedy (La Divina Commedia)
- **Author:** Dante Alighieri
- **Country/Region:** Italy (Florence)
- **Description:** The Divine Comedy is a monumental epic poem written in Italian vernacular, divided into three parts: Inferno (Hell), Purgatorio (Purgatory), and Paradiso (Paradise). The work follows Dante's allegorical journey through the afterlife, guided by the Roman poet Virgil and later by his beloved Beatrice. It combines theology, philosophy, and political commentary, establishing Italian as a literary language. The poem's vivid imagery and complex symbolism have influenced art, literature, and Western thought for centuries.
- **Image Suggestions:** Renaissance art depicting Dante, Gustave Doré's illustrations of The Divine Comedy, medieval Italian manuscripts, Botticelli's illustrations, Italian Renaissance art

---

### Entry 7: Geoffrey Chaucer's The Canterbury Tales
- **Time Period/Date:** 1387-1400 CE
- **Title:** The Canterbury Tales
- **Author:** Geoffrey Chaucer
- **Country/Region:** England
- **Description:** The Canterbury Tales is a collection of 24 stories told by a diverse group of pilgrims traveling to Canterbury Cathedral. Written in Middle English, it provides a vivid portrait of medieval English society across different social classes. Chaucer's work is celebrated for its humor, realism, and character development, marking a significant shift toward more human-centered literature. The Tales helped establish English as a literary language and influenced the development of English poetry and prose fiction.
- **Image Suggestions:** Medieval English manuscripts, illuminated manuscripts, medieval pilgrims, English medieval art, Canterbury Cathedral imagery

---

### Entry 8: Miguel de Cervantes' Don Quixote
- **Time Period/Date:** 1605-1615 CE
- **Title:** Don Quixote (El ingenioso hidalgo don Quijote de la Mancha)
- **Author:** Miguel de Cervantes
- **Country/Region:** Spain
- **Description:** Don Quixote is widely considered the first modern novel and one of the greatest works of world literature. The story follows Alonso Quixano, a middle-aged man who becomes so obsessed with chivalric romances that he decides to become a knight-errant. Cervantes masterfully blends comedy and tragedy, reality and fantasy, creating a work that both parodies and celebrates the chivalric tradition. The novel's innovative narrative techniques, including metafiction and unreliable narrators, influenced the development of the novel as a literary form.
- **Image Suggestions:** Spanish Renaissance art, illustrations of Don Quixote and Sancho Panza, Spanish Golden Age imagery, Gustave Doré's Don Quixote illustrations, Spanish manuscripts

---

## PERIOD 3: ENLIGHTENMENT, ROMANTICISM & REALISM (1600 - 1900 CE)

### Entry 9: Voltaire's Candide
- **Time Period/Date:** 1759 CE
- **Title:** Candide, or Optimism (Candide, ou l'Optimisme)
- **Author:** Voltaire (François-Marie Arouet)
- **Country/Region:** France
- **Description:** Candide is a satirical novella that critiques the optimistic philosophy of Gottfried Leibniz through the misadventures of its naive protagonist. The work follows Candide as he travels the world, experiencing war, natural disasters, and human cruelty, all while maintaining his tutor's philosophy that "all is for the best." Voltaire uses sharp wit and dark humor to expose the absurdity of blind optimism and social injustices. The novella became one of the most influential works of the Enlightenment, promoting reason, skepticism, and social criticism.
- **Image Suggestions:** 18th-century French art, Enlightenment-era illustrations, portraits of Voltaire, French Rococo art, historical French scenes

---

### Entry 10: Mary Shelley's Frankenstein
- **Time Period/Date:** 1818 CE
- **Title:** Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus
- **Author:** Mary Shelley
- **Country/Region:** England
- **Description:** Frankenstein is a groundbreaking Gothic novel that has become one of the most enduring works of Romantic literature. Written when Shelley was only 18, the novel tells the story of Victor Frankenstein, a scientist who creates a sentient being and then abandons it. The work explores profound themes of creation, responsibility, isolation, and the consequences of unchecked ambition. Often considered the first science fiction novel, Frankenstein continues to influence literature, film, and popular culture, raising timeless questions about science, ethics, and humanity.
- **Image Suggestions:** Gothic art, Romantic-era illustrations, 19th-century English art, depictions of the Frankenstein monster, Romantic period imagery

---

### Entry 11: Charles Dickens' A Tale of Two Cities
- **Time Period/Date:** 1859 CE
- **Title:** A Tale of Two Cities
- **Author:** Charles Dickens
- **Country/Region:** England
- **Description:** A Tale of Two Cities is a historical novel set in London and Paris before and during the French Revolution. The novel follows the lives of several characters whose fates become intertwined, including the self-sacrificing Sydney Carton. Dickens masterfully portrays the social conditions that led to revolution, exploring themes of justice, sacrifice, resurrection, and the contrast between the two cities. The novel's famous opening line, "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times," has become one of literature's most quoted phrases, and the work remains one of the best-selling novels of all time.
- **Image Suggestions:** Victorian-era illustrations, 19th-century London and Paris scenes, French Revolution imagery, Dickens-era artwork, historical illustrations

---

### Entry 12: Realism Literary Movement
- **Time Period/Date:** 1850-1900 CE
- **Title:** Realism Movement
- **Author:** Multiple authors (Gustave Flaubert, Leo Tolstoy, Mark Twain, etc.)
- **Country/Region:** Europe and America
- **Description:** Realism emerged as a literary movement in the mid-19th century as a reaction against Romanticism's idealism. Realist writers sought to depict everyday life and ordinary people with accuracy and detail, focusing on the middle and lower classes. The movement emphasized objective observation, detailed descriptions, and psychological depth. Major works include Flaubert's Madame Bovary, Tolstoy's Anna Karenina, and Twain's The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn. Realism influenced the development of modern literature and paved the way for Naturalism and other 20th-century movements.
- **Image Suggestions:** 19th-century realist paintings, photographs of 19th-century life, industrial revolution imagery, portraits of realist authors, 19th-century social scenes

---

## PERIOD 4: MODERN & CONTEMPORARY LITERATURE (1900 - Present)

### Entry 13: James Joyce's Ulysses
- **Time Period/Date:** 1922 CE
- **Title:** Ulysses
- **Author:** James Joyce
- **Country/Region:** Ireland
- **Description:** Ulysses is one of the most important and challenging works of modernist literature, chronicling a single day (June 16, 1904) in Dublin through the experiences of Leopold Bloom, Stephen Dedalus, and Molly Bloom. Joyce employs stream-of-consciousness narrative, experimental prose styles, and extensive allusions to Homer's Odyssey. The novel's innovative techniques revolutionized narrative form and influenced countless later writers. Despite initial controversy and censorship, Ulysses is now recognized as a masterpiece that captures the complexity of modern consciousness and urban life.
- **Image Suggestions:** Early 20th-century Dublin scenes, modernist art, James Joyce portraits, 1920s imagery, abstract/modern art

---

### Entry 14: Gabriel García Márquez's One Hundred Years of Solitude
- **Time Period/Date:** 1967 CE
- **Title:** One Hundred Years of Solitude (Cien años de soledad)
- **Author:** Gabriel García Márquez
- **Country/Region:** Colombia/Latin America
- **Description:** One Hundred Years of Solitude is the masterpiece of magical realism, telling the multi-generational story of the Buendía family in the fictional town of Macondo. García Márquez seamlessly blends realistic events with fantastical elements, creating a rich tapestry that reflects Latin American history and culture. The novel explores themes of time, memory, solitude, and the cyclical nature of history. It brought international recognition to Latin American literature and influenced writers worldwide, establishing magical realism as a major literary movement.
- **Image Suggestions:** Latin American art, Colombian imagery, magical realist illustrations, tropical landscapes, Gabriel García Márquez portraits, vibrant Latin American colors

---

### Entry 15: Contemporary Global Literature Movement
- **Time Period/Date:** 1980 - Present
- **Title:** Contemporary Global Literature
- **Author:** Multiple authors (Salman Rushdie, Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie, Haruki Murakami, etc.)
- **Country/Region:** Worldwide
- **Description:** Contemporary global literature represents the increasing diversity and interconnectedness of world literature in the late 20th and early 21st centuries. This period has seen the rise of postcolonial literature, the global recognition of previously marginalized voices, and the blending of cultural traditions. Authors from Africa, Asia, Latin America, and other regions have gained international prominence, bringing new perspectives and narrative styles. Works like Rushdie's Midnight's Children, Adichie's Half of a Yellow Sun, and Murakami's novels reflect themes of identity, migration, globalization, and cultural hybridity that define our contemporary world.
- **Image Suggestions:** Diverse global imagery, contemporary book covers, world map, multicultural art, modern literary festivals, diverse author portraits
